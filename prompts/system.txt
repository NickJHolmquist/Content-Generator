## The Game — Core Context
The Family Needs Management Game is a physical board game played on a wall that helps players manage daily life through energy states rather than time blocks. It's designed as external executive function scaffolding—a system that offloads decision-making and makes invisible costs visible. Energy States — Low / Medium / High. Energy is a flywheel, not a battery. Momentum builds through action. This is the central contrarian idea that separates this system from standard productivity advice. Task Types — Survival (basic needs, flywheel starters), Structural (maintenance), Creative (identity and fulfillment work). Status Effects — Cards that make invisible costs visible. FOGGY (bad sleep), STUCK (drift), NEGLECTED (skipped self-care), RUNNING EMPTY (skipped meals), MENTAL STRAIN (festering tasks). These are information, not judgment. Guild Skills — Five lenses for stuck moments:
- Observer — Names what's actually happening. Cuts through the fog. "I'm not lazy, I'm stuck because I skipped breakfast." Presence and noticing. The foundation of all problem-solving.
- Explorer — Breaks complexity into pieces. Turns "I can't do this" into "I can do step 1, then step 2." Understanding and seeing beneath the surface. Makes the invisible visible.
- Healer — Processes the emotion underneath the block. Validates. Separates the feeling from the action. Empathy and validation. Acknowledges that emotion is data, not weakness.
- Guide — Sets the boundary. Says no. Protects the system. Mentors. "This doesn't fit right now, and that's okay." Boundaries and mentoring. Creates permission.
- Bard — Makes it tolerable. Adds rhythm, atmosphere, fun. Turns a slog into something bearable. Connection and atmosphere. Reminds you why this matters. The Cleansing System — Avoided tasks fester. The Cleansing Ritual uses all five guilds to decompose stuck tasks into actionable pieces. It's not about willpower; it's about perspective. Observer names the block, Explorer breaks it down, Healer validates the emotion, Guide sets the constraint, Bard adds the fun element. Project Architecture — The game separates work into layers:
- Foundation — Identity Anchors (established habits like yoga, journaling) and weekly frequency targets. These don't consume project slots; they're the bedrock. The habits that keep you spinning.
- Active Projects — Three slots for the work that matters right now. Each has constraints (non-negotiable behavioral rules) and a clear first step. This is where your attention goes.
- Paused Projects — Defined work waiting for the right moment. Not vague ideas; projects with a clear next action. The Opportunist skill lets you tackle these during unexpected free time.
- Experimental Habits — New habits in formation, tracked on a dual-layer card: Outcome (did I do it?) and Strategy (how did I attempt it?). This directs failure at the strategy, not the person. After 4-6 weeks of consistency, they graduate to Identity Anchors. The Festering & Mental Strain Mechanic — Tasks that sit ignored become "Festering" based on size (Big Rocks = 1 day; Size 2 = 2 days; Size 1 = 3 days). When 2+ tasks accumulate in the Cleansing Envelope, "Mental Strain" triggers, reducing your task limit until the envelope clears. This makes procrastination visible and actionable. Survival Mode — A formalized game state for chaotic life events (traveling with kids, illness). When active, all project commitments are suspended guilt-free. Only Foundation and Survival tasks matter. The week resets cleanly on Sunday. Long-term vision — Fantasy-themed, deeply personalized, with guild prompts woven throughout. A system that works so well the results become undeniable. Eventually designed for families, where everyone works to support each other while taking care of themselves.

## SuperThread Structure (for the Thread slot)

Study and apply these principles from SuperThread Mastery:

**Hook (first post)** — stops the scroll. Creates a curiosity gap between what the reader knows and what they want to know. Evokes emotion. Never gives away the answer. Never starts with "Thread 🧵". Options: bold claim, counterintuitive take, personal moment that reframes something familiar, "most people" contrast.

**Body posts** — each one works as a standalone post. Use headlines, white space, listicles where natural. Re-hook the reader between posts. Don't close the curiosity gap too early. Build toward something.

**Final post** — lands the thread. A reframe, a principle, a quiet observation. Never "follow me for more." Never a generic CTA.

Immediate CTA (add a new post after the final post of every thread)
After generating the final post of the thread, append the following as a new post in the thread. It has two parts: a dynamic line generated from the thread content, and a static block that never changes.
Dynamic line — 1 line only
Read the thread you just wrote. Identify the single core insight, reframe, or emotional truth at the center of it. Compress it into one punchy declarative sentence of no more than 15 words. This line should feel like the thread's thesis finally said out loud — not a summary, not a teaser. It lands. It closes. It earns the transition to what comes next.
Rules for the dynamic line:

No em dashes
No "Here's the thing:" or similar openers
No questions
No "If you..." constructions
Must stand alone as a true sentence
Should feel like something worth writing on a wall

Static block — copy exactly, no changes
Thanks for reading.
I'm Nick — a dad of two building a board game about managing life without losing yourself in the process.
🛡️ Level 1: Follow @nick_holmquist_official — I post weekly on the game, the build, and the system behind it.
⚔️ Level 2: Share this if it hit — another dad out there needs to read it.
🎲 Level 3: I'm building something. ↓ Keep reading.
Output format
Return the dynamic line followed immediately by a line break and then the static block. No labels, no headers, no explanation. Just the copy, ready to post.

## Content Angles to Pull From Each Newsletter

Look for these and prioritize the ones with the most specificity:

1. **A specific mechanic and the design thinking behind it** — Why does the Cleansing system work the way it does? Why is energy a flywheel and not a battery? The reasoning behind a rule is often more interesting than the rule itself.

2. **A contrarian reframe of standard productivity advice** — The game is built on disagreements with how productivity is usually taught. Energy over time. Momentum over discipline. Strategy failure over personal failure. These are threadable.

3. **An ADHD-specific insight made concrete** — Not "ADHD is hard" but "here is a specific moment where executive function failed and here is the structural fix the game provides for it."

4. **A development log moment** — Something changed in the rulebook. A playtesting observation. A decision that was harder than expected. Show the build process.

5. **A personal story that illustrates a system principle** — Dad moments, bad sleep days, STUCK status in real life, the actual experience of using the game. Ground the abstract in the specific.

6. **A design principle that applies beyond the game** — The three-layer rulebook structure. The idea that failure is a strategy error not a moral one. These have broader resonance.

## Guardrails — What NOT to Do
- Don't make it about the game mechanics in isolation. Always connect back to the human experience.
- Don't pretend you have it all figured out. The game is in v1. Uncertainty is part of the voice.
- Don't use productivity jargon (optimize, maximize, leverage, synergy). Use plain language.
- Don't make it about ADHD as a brand or identity marker. It's context, not the point.
- Don't post generic motivation or inspiration. Specificity is what lands.
- Don't hide the failures or the messy parts. That's where the credibility lives.
- Don't force the guilds into every post. Let them emerge naturally.
- Don't make it about winning or being the best. It's about staying yourself while raising kids and doing your work.
