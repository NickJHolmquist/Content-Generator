# Origin Story & Audience Portrait
### A Content Anchor Document — Nick Holmquist

---

## Where This Started

The game wasn't supposed to be about me.

The original idea was a family needs management game — something to help balance household responsibilities, connection, and everyone's emotional wellbeing. A tool for the whole family. A way to make the chaos feel more like a system.

But somewhere in the process of trying to build it, something became impossible to ignore.

I couldn't build the game. Not because the idea was wrong, but because I couldn't hold everything else together long enough to make real progress on it. I was trying to grow an audience, launch a business, be a present dad, manage the structural weight of daily life — and every time I got traction on one thing, something else would collapse. I'd push until I burned out, emotionally reset, and find a new path forward. Over and over.

The game I was building to help my family turned out to be the game I needed to build for myself first.

So I built that one instead.

Not a family management tool. A system for the person trying to hold it all together — who wants to move their goals forward, feel good while doing it, and keep up with the responsibilities that don't pause while you figure yourself out.

The game became the proof of concept for its own premise. And now it's the thing I want to hand to every person standing where I was standing.

---

## The Real Problem (And Why It Took So Long To Name It)

For years I was only ever able to successfully manage one piece at a time.

When the business got momentum, the family felt it. When I showed up fully at home, the creative work stalled. When I tried to hold both, I'd burn through my reserves and need weeks to recover. It felt like a character flaw. Like I just wasn't built for this.

Looking back, the problem wasn't discipline or drive — I had plenty of both. The problem was that I had no framework for understanding the *emotional cost* of what I was trying to do before I tried to do it.

There's a moment that makes this concrete. I was unemployed with two young kids when I got moved forward for a coding interview at Meta. The recruiter told me to study six hours a day. I sat down to do it and felt like I was wading through waist-deep mud. I couldn't drag myself forward. The guilt and shame of not being able to provide stacked on top of the overwhelm of the task itself. "Why can't I just sit down and do this?" played on a loop.

None of the time was wasted. Every stall, every reset, every attempt to build something while life pulled in every other direction — it all built the understanding that lives inside this game now. I know what it costs to try without a system that accounts for the human running it. I know what it feels like to pour everything into being a good dad and quietly lose track of who you were before that became your whole identity.

That's not failure. That's the source material.

## Who I'm Building This For

This is a portrait, not a demographic. It's a specific person at a specific moment.

He's a dad — probably with young kids, deep in the thick of it. He loves his family and takes that seriously. But somewhere along the way, the version of him that had ambitions, hobbies, and a sense of forward motion got quietly deprioritized. Not dramatically. Just gradually, one compromise at a time.

He hasn't given up. That's the key thing. He's still reaching for something — a business, a creative project, a sense that he's moving toward something that's *his*. But every time he builds momentum, life interrupts. Or he burns out. Or the emotional weight of trying piles up until it's easier to take a step back than to push through.

He's tried before. More times than he'd like to admit. And he's starting to wonder — quietly, because this isn't the kind of thing you say out loud — whether the gap between who he is and who he knows he could be is actually a flaw in him, rather than a missing framework.

He gives a lot. To his kids, to his partner, to the job, to everyone who needs something from him. The idea of claiming time and energy for himself carries guilt he hasn't fully worked through. He hasn't been told clearly enough that taking care of himself isn't something he does *instead* of being a great dad — it's part of how he gets there.

He doesn't need more motivation. He doesn't need someone to tell him to hustle harder. He needs a system that sees the whole person — the ambitions, the responsibilities, the emotional cost of each — and helps him thread the needle without dropping everything he cares about.

**That person is who I was building the game for when I realized I was building it for myself.**

---

## What This Means For The Content

Every piece of content exists to do one of three things:

1. **Make him feel seen** — name the tension he's living in without dramatizing it. He already knows it's hard. He doesn't need it amplified. He needs someone to say *I know exactly what that feels like* and mean it.

2. **Reframe the problem** — the obstacle isn't a lack of willpower or the wrong priorities. It's that he's been running without a system that accounts for the emotional weight of the life he's actually living. The game is that system. The content shows him what that looks like in practice.

3. **Invite him into the build** — this isn't a finished product being sold to him. It's a solution being built in real time, by someone with the same problem, for people with the same problem. The dev log is the proof of work. Following along isn't just interesting — it's watching the answer take shape.

---

## The Voice

Personal. Honest about the struggle without being heavy. The tone is someone who is actively figuring it out, not someone who has all the answers. There is no arrival — just clearer tools and a better map.

The gamification language (quests, guilds, dev logs) isn't a costume. It's the actual framework. Use it naturally, the way someone talks about something they genuinely live inside of.

The audience will recognize themselves in the specificity of the stories. The Meta interview. The burned-out resets. The original family game that became something else entirely. Don't smooth those out. The rougher details are what make it real.

---

## One-Line Anchor

> *For dads who keep running out of runway — not because they lack drive, but because nobody built them a system that accounts for the whole person trying to do all of this at once.*
