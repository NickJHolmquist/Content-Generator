# Dev log #10: Experimental Habits
I had big ambitions to get a full rulebook pumped out this week and big surprise...
That didn't happen.
Instead though I discovered something deeper that I didn't even realize I was searching for.
A structure to the rulebook that serves people individually.
This is what brought about the 3 different layers: the mechanics (how the game works), the engagement (what keeps players coming back) and the personal connection (the very essence of what makes it yours).
Every key part of the game needs all three. The first mechanic to go through this process is one I desperately needed.
I didn't realize going into this that these layers were going to result in every mechanic requiring a personal connection to the player that makes the game work.
Without a player's personal projects, goals and struggles the game will not work.
I recognized a gap in my game when I had no clean way to support the development of new long term creative goals like writing my novel.
I needed a mechanic to boil down a goal into its first key habit.
This is where the idea of experimental habits came from, a way to bridge the gap between goal and action.
But here's the thing, a habit without a reason is a habit that does not stick.
Habits must be tied to either a project or an identity level goal.
It should be something that either moves your projects forward or directly improves your day to day life in some way.
Without this understanding I find the motivation isn't there to tackle it.
Through this process it occurred to me I've been subconsciously going through the experimental habit process already with my newsletter writing goals.
I've mentioned before how I've moved my writing to earlier in the week but that's not all I've done.
The strategy behind the 'write newsletter by Wednesday' goal is broken down further, ideate on Monday, write rough draft on Tuesday and polish and schedule on Wednesday.
I've been thinking of this as the dual track habit development method.
The first track is about 'did I do the thing?'.
While the second track is about 'how will I do the thing?'.
And then there is actually another layer to consider after you get to where you're successfully doing the thing by nailing down the how through understanding and that is the higher level consideration of 'is this thing accomplishing what I wanted it to do for this project or my life?'.
The biggest benefit by far of this method is the understanding that habits are modular.
If you 'fail' to do the habit that doesn't make you a bad or lazy person, it simply means you need to evaluate each track of your habit and figure out where the break is.
For example with my newsletter I've pretty much always wanted it done earlier in the week.
That is not new, what is new is the fact that I have reached a point of clarity where I know that writing my newsletter is the first major creative goal of the week.
This has cleared my mind and made it much easier to sit down and 'do the thing'.
In addition I've realized that 'failing' to meet that goal doesn't make me 'lazy' or 'bad at sticking to schedules', it simply means I had a break in my habit structure or life just got in the way.
I'm writing this on Thursday as we speak a day past when it was 'due'.
This still is not my preferred situation but I've come to accept that with young kids and a rapidly developing game I can't always strictly hold to a schedule.
Instead of beating myself up I'm accepting where I'm at and continuing to evaluate the success of this new habit.
Which has still been a huge success.
A big part of this goal for me is to reduce stress and increase enjoyment in my work and man it feels so good to get to think deeply about the direction this project is heading in and not feel rushed to pump out the week's newsletter.
I've been feeling progressively lighter as I offload my goals and ideas into my game.
There is something profoundly motivating about seeing your projects on the wall and investing conscious effort each week in plotting your path forward.
What habit do you feel drawn to experiment with?
